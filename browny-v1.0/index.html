Major App Coding Project

We initially were going to create a system where a patient books an appointment online, walks in and gets their face scanned by a camera. Then their medical history and their appointment details would show up on a computer monitored by a secretary. This idea stemmed from our experiences with long lines at clinics, emergency care, etc. Especially in Canada where healthcare is free, this seemed to solve a real world issue. However this made lots of sense theoretically, but practically, it was impossible. Finding a framework to help match what we want didn’t work. We would have to get access to every citizen’s files in Canada from the government to make it work. If not, we can just use the information they fill out online. However, how will we know that the information is accurate? So we scratched the idea and moved on to a program that helps with posture detection. Trying to stay on the topic of solving real life issues. This idea really came to surface after the Environmental Impact Presentation. We went into detail on the effect computers have on us mentally and physically. Computers cause poor posture like slouching/hunching, bad screen alignment, and incorrect use of chairs which can affect your muscle joints, and musculoskeletal issues. Spending long hours on computers like 7+ hours will create stiffness and muscle soreness. This causes lack of movement because you are maintaining one position with little to no breaks. The brightness (poor lighting, blue light exposure, and screen resolution) and viewing distance (font size, screen and position) creates eye strain. 68% of people without an ergonomic setup have had musculoskeletal injuries which is potentially caused by doing the same task repeatedly, causing stress on nerves and tendons. 20% of people without an ergonomic setup have had carpal tunnel syndrome which is a nerve in the wrist that is used repeatedly with wrong equipment. 60-85% of people without an ergonomic setup have muscle strain. We came to the conclusion that your ergonomic workstation should have solutions to all the physical, posture related problems that it is causing. First, the screen needs to be tilted slightly upward with the top of the screen at eye-level, and around 45-60cm away. Second, the environment needs to meet your needs, including the air circulation, temperature, cleanliness, and room for your legs. Third, the chair needs to be able to recline slightly, helping you to sit back causing your thighs to be parallel to the floor. Fourth, the keyboard and mouse need to be at elbow height with your fingers making a 90 degree angle, using your elbows and not your wrist to maneuver around, and having your shoulders relaxed. However, this is easier said than done. A study showed that someone on their laptop who really tried not to touch their face with their hands (study was done during covid to prevent the spread), ended up touching their face 16 times in one hour. Trying to fix a really strong habit is almost impossible, so my group and I created something that will remind you that your posture needs to be fixed.

Our program is pretty straightforward. It uses your camera system in your computer to calculate the distance between you and the camera using the parameters of your head and shoulders. If that distance is relatively far (if distance < 0.5), then you are not hunching over and your posture is good. When it is between a certain distance (if distance=0.5), the video feed flashes yellow, giving you a warning that your posture needs to be fixed. If the distance is very minimal (very close to the screen and if distance> 0.5), the screen flashes red with lots of red text on the screen, and plays a very loud noise. This only happens after 1-2 minutes because we are aware sometimes you just want to see something close up or point at the screen to show something. We showed it to Mr. Brown in class, and it is in fact very obnoxious. You can see a detailed summary of your posture in Real-Time Plot. This creates a graph comparing distance with time. Using the parameters, it calculates the distance and tells you when you are slouching. This is a separate application that runs in the background.

    In this directory, we are calculating the x and y parameters using 2 points for the y values and 2 points for the x value. The program finds the distance between each x and y and returns a positive value between 0-1. However, if one doesn’t exist at the moment, then it returns a negative value. Then using the double functions and if statement, it multiples each value by 2, checks if they are valid (if not, then returns and error value), then calculates the distance. x2 - x1 and y2 - y1 is calculating the difference between the x and y values. std::pow(x2 - x1, 2) and std::pow(y2 - y1, 2) is computing the squares of those values. std::pow(x2 - x1, 2) + std::pow(y2 - y1, 2) adds the squares of the differences. According to the Pythagorean theorem, the sum of the squares of the differences in the coordinates gives the square of the distance between the two points. std::sqrt(...) computes the square root of the value inside the parentheses. Taking the square root of the sum of the squares gives the Euclidean distance between the two points.

Coding Reflection

Past

With my previous knowledge of coding, I only knew how to make websites with HTML, CSS and Javascript, along with knowledge gained from Hack Club with Python. I was able to make fun little games like hangman and a casino style slot machine game.

Present

The coding project that we brainstormed required frameworks. Frameworks that are cross platform and are multi-modal (video, audio, and sensor data). MediaPipe was perfect for that. It is used for real-time perception like estimating live, hand tracking, face detection, and other vision applications. MediaPipe is designed to perform real-time processing on both mobile devices and desktops. The framework is highly modular, allowing developers to use pre-built components (such as pose estimation or object detection models) or create their own custom components. MediaPipe supports multiple platforms, including Android, iOS, web, and desktop (Windows, macOS, and Linux). Learning what MediaPipe is and how to use it was my biggest accomplishment during the project because it has opened the world to a bunch of other frameworks that I can use for future projects.

Future

I have an idea of creating frameworks that benefit me in specific projects and the public as well. The level of complexity of future projects have risen a lot, but I enjoy the challenge. In the future, I want to incorporate AI into my projects. Similar to almost every application nowadays, AI has been more popular than ever. I would have to understand the basics of AI, choosing the right tools and frameworks, finding the purpose for the AI, developing a model and training, integrating, deploying and maintaining it.
